What is Phishing?

Attacks that impersonate trusted sources to trick you into revealing credentials, payment info, or personal data. Below — a concise, practical view: how it works, why it works, examples, and what to do.

How phishing works — quick flow

  1. Target selection — attacker chooses mass audience or specific person.
  2. Lure crafting — message mimics a trusted sender (bank, colleague, app).
  3. Delivery — email, SMS, social DM, or phone call.
  4. Action — click link, open attachment, or enter credentials.
  5. Exploitation — credential theft, malware, account takeover.

Tip: Treat unexpected requests for credentials or money as suspicious — verify separately.

Why phishing succeeds — quick psychology

  • Authority — messages seem to come from someone in charge.
  • Urgency — fear of losing access or money forces fast clicks.
  • Familiarity — logos, templates, and personal info lower guard.
  • Convenience — people take the path of least resistance (click).

Counter: pause, breathe, and verify before acting — that 5-second check prevents most attacks.

Common real-world examples

Bank alert (Email)

Message claims account issue and includes a link to “verify.” Real banks don’t ask passwords via email.

Check: hover links, inspect domain, call bank number from official site.

Delivery / OTP (SMS)

Text asks to confirm details or provide OTP. Scammers reuse OTPs to hijack accounts.

Check: open courier’s official app or site instead of clicking SMS links.

Spear / Executive Fraud

Targeted email that looks internal (CEO, vendor) asking for urgent payment or data.

Check: verify with a quick call to a known number (not reply email).

Practical checklist (do this now)

  • Pause before clicking any unexpected link.
  • Hover links on desktop; long-press on mobile to preview URL.
  • Verify sender using official contact methods.
  • Enable 2FA and use a password manager.

Quick 5-second checks

  • Is the sender domain exact or slightly off?
  • Does the message pressure you to act now?
  • Are there unexpected attachments or requests for secrets?
  • Does the tone or spelling feel unprofessional?

If multiple checks fail → report as phishing and delete.
